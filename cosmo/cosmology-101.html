Cosmology 101

The universe is 13.8 billion years old, 93 billion light-years wide, mostly made of stuff we can't see — and the more precisely we measure it, the stranger it gets. Here's the whole story, the settled science and the beautiful unsolved paradoxes, in one scrollable page.

Chapter 2 · Deep time

13.8 billion years, top to bottom

From a fraction of a fraction of a second to the formation of your solar system. The early universe happened breathtakingly fast; the interesting-for-life part came very, very late.

t ≈ 10⁻³⁴ s · Inflation

The universe balloons

Space expands by a factor of at least 10²⁶ in an instant, smoothing everything out and stretching quantum ripples into the seeds of future galaxies.

t ≈ 10⁻⁶ s · Quark soup freezes

Protons & neutrons form

Quarks bind into the first protons and neutrons. A slight excess of matter over antimatter — about one part in a billion — survives. That leftover is everything.

t ≈ 3 min · Nucleosynthesis

The first elements

Hydrogen, helium and a trace of lithium are forged. The cosmic recipe — roughly 75% hydrogen, 25% helium — is locked in and matches observation stunningly.

t ≈ 380,000 yr · Recombination

The universe turns transparent

It cools enough for electrons to join nuclei into atoms. Light finally streams free — the flash we still detect today as the cosmic microwave background.

t ≈ 100–500 Myr · Cosmic dawn

First stars ignite

Gravity gathers gas into the first stars and galaxies, ending the "cosmic dark ages." JWST is now finding these earlier and brighter than models predicted.

t ≈ 9.2 Gyr · The Sun forms

Our neighborhood assembles

About 4.6 billion years ago, a cloud of gas enriched by earlier stellar generations collapses into the Sun and planets. You are made of that recycled stardust.

t ≈ 13.8 Gyr · Now

Dark energy takes over

For the last ~5 billion years the expansion has been accelerating. Distant galaxies are receding ever faster, and eventually most will vanish beyond our horizon forever.

Chapter 3 · The cosmic recipe

What is the universe actually made of?

Here's the humbling headline: everything humanity has ever seen, touched, or built — every star, planet and person — is about 5% of the total. The rest is two kinds of darkness we've named but not identified.

Dark energy · ~68% — a repulsive "pressure of empty space" driving accelerating expansion. Cause unknown.
Dark matter · ~27% — invisible mass that outweighs stars ~5:1 and holds galaxies together. Never directly detected.
Ordinary matter · ~5% — atoms: stars, gas, planets, you. And most of this is thin intergalactic gas, not stars.

We infer dark matter from how galaxies rotate and how light bends around them (gravitational lensing), and dark energy from how the expansion speeds up. Both are among the biggest open problems in physics.

Chapter 4 · The expanding universe

Space itself is stretching

Galaxies aren't flying through space away from us — the space between them is growing, like raisins drifting apart in rising dough. The farther a galaxy is, the faster it recedes. That's Hubble's Law, and it's the single observation that gave us the Big Bang.

Chapter 6 · The baby picture

The oldest light in existence

When the universe was 380,000 years old it cooled enough to become transparent, releasing a flash of light in every direction. Stretched by 13.8 billion years of expansion, that flash is now faint microwaves at 2.725 K — the cosmic microwave background (CMB). It's about 1% of the static on an old untuned TV.

The CMB is astonishingly uniform, but its tiny temperature ripples (1 part in 100,000) are the fingerprints of the density variations that grew into every galaxy. Measuring them precisely is how we pinned down the age, geometry, and contents of the cosmos.

Chapter 7 · The good stuff

Seven paradoxes cosmologists argue about

This is where cosmology stops being a textbook and becomes a frontier. Tap any card to flip it.

Paradox · 1823

Why is the night sky dark?

Olbers' Paradox: in an infinite, eternal, star-filled universe every line of sight should end on a star, so the whole sky should blaze like the Sun. It doesn't.

tap for the resolution →
Resolution

Because it had a beginning

The universe is finite in age, so light from very distant stars hasn't arrived yet. And expansion redshifts distant light out of the visible band. Darkness itself is evidence of the Big Bang.

Crisis · active

The Hubble Tension

Measure the expansion rate H₀ from the early universe (Planck/CMB) and you get ~67.4. Measure it from nearby supernovae (SH0ES) and you get ~73. Both are precise. They disagree at 5σ.

tap for the state of play →
2025 status

JWST made it worse

Many hoped Webb would show the local measurement was flawed. Instead it confirmed the Cepheid distances, deepening the tension. It may be pointing to genuinely new physics in the early universe.

Frontier · 2024–25

Is dark energy changing?

The simplest model treats dark energy as a constant (Einstein's Λ). But the DESI survey now sees hints that it may have been stronger in the past and is weakening.

tap for the numbers →
DESI DR2

A 4σ hint

Combining DESI's 2025 data with the CMB and supernovae disfavors a constant Λ at up to ~4σ. Not yet the 5σ "discovery" bar — but if it holds, the textbook model needs rewriting.

Paradox · 1950

Where is everybody?

The Fermi Paradox: the galaxy is old and huge, with billions of Earth-like worlds. A single civilization could colonize it in a few million years. So why total silence?

tap for the candidates →
Possible answers

Take your pick

Maybe life is rare; maybe intelligence self-destructs (a "Great Filter"); maybe they're out there but quiet, or we're too early to the party. We genuinely don't know — and that uncertainty is itself alarming.

Paradox · quantum

Black-hole information

Hawking showed black holes evaporate. But if they radiate featureless heat and vanish, the information about whatever fell in is destroyed — which quantum mechanics forbids.

tap for the twist →
Where it stands

Physics vs. physics

General relativity and quantum theory give contradictory answers, so one must bend. Recent work ("islands," the Page curve) suggests information does escape, but a full theory of quantum gravity is still missing.

Paradox · fine-tuning

The worst prediction in physics

Quantum theory predicts the energy of empty space should be up to 10¹²⁰ times larger than the tiny dark-energy value we observe. It's the largest mismatch between theory and reality ever.

tap for the ideas →
Attempts

No accepted answer

Proposals range from unknown cancellation mechanisms to the multiverse — that Λ varies across countless universes and we live in a rare one where it's small enough for galaxies (and us) to form.

Paradox · origin

Why is there anything at all?

The Big Bang should have made matter and antimatter in equal amounts, which annihilate completely. Yet here we are. A one-in-a-billion asymmetry tipped the balance toward matter.

tap for the search →
Baryogenesis

An unsolved excess

Something in the early universe favored matter over antimatter. Experiments hunt for tiny asymmetries in particle decays that could explain it, but the mechanism remains one of physics' great open questions.

Frontier · JWST

The "impossible" early galaxies

Webb keeps finding galaxies that look too big, too bright and too mature to exist just a few hundred million years after the Big Bang.

tap for what it means →
Open question

Faster than we thought

Either early star formation was far more efficient than models allow, or some of the light comes from feeding black holes. It's an active puzzle reshaping our picture of cosmic dawn — not (yet) a crisis for the Big Bang.

A 60-second glossary

Redshift (z) — how much expansion has stretched light on its way to us.
Parsec / Mpc — 3.26 light-years; a megaparsec is a million of those.
ΛCDM — the standard model: Lambda (dark energy) + Cold Dark Matter.
Scale factor a(t) — the "size" of the universe relative to today (a=1 now).
Baryonic matter — ordinary atoms, as opposed to dark matter.
Light-year — the distance light travels in a year, ~9.46 trillion km.
BAO — baryon acoustic oscillations, a "standard ruler" frozen into galaxy spacing.
Gravitational lensing — mass bending light; our best map of dark matter.

Everything here reflects the consensus of modern cosmology as of 2026 — including the honest admission that ~95% of the universe remains a mystery. The best time to be curious about the cosmos is always now.
