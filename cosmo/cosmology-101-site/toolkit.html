Redshift → lookback time

Light from distant galaxies is stretched (redshifted). Higher redshift z = older light. This computes lookback time by numerically integrating the Friedmann equation for a flat ΛCDM universe (H₀ = 67.4, Ωm = 0.315, ΩΛ = 0.685).

A 90-second glossary

Redshift (z) — how much cosmic expansion has stretched light on its way to us; a proxy for distance and lookback time.
Parsec / Mpc — a parsec is 3.26 light-years; a megaparsec (Mpc) is a million of them.
Light-year — the distance light travels in one year, about 9.46 trillion km.
ΛCDM — the standard model of cosmology: Lambda (dark energy) + Cold Dark Matter.
Scale factor a(t) — the relative "size" of the universe; a = 1 today, smaller in the past.
Hubble constant (H₀) — the present expansion rate, in km/s per megaparsec.
Baryonic matter — ordinary atoms (protons & neutrons), as opposed to dark matter.
CMB — cosmic microwave background, the 2.725 K afterglow of the young universe.
BAO — baryon acoustic oscillations, frozen sound waves used as a cosmic "standard ruler."
Gravitational lensing — mass bending light; our best tool for mapping dark matter.
Equation of state (w) — the ratio of pressure to energy density; w = −1 for a cosmological constant.
Recombination — when the universe cooled enough to form neutral atoms and become transparent (~380,000 yr).
