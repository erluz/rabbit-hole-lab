The Cosmic Timeline

13.8 billion years, from the first sliver of a second to the world outside your window. The early universe changed almost unimaginably fast: more happened in its first three minutes than in the billions of years since. The part that includes stars, planets, and life came astonishingly late.

A note on time. "t" is time since the Big Bang. Powers of ten matter enormously here — 10⁻³⁴ s is a trillionth of a trillionth of a trillionth of a second, while 10⁹ yr is a billion years. The scale is logarithmic, not linear.

The very early universe

The first second. Physics here runs on energies far beyond any particle accelerator, so parts are informed theory rather than direct observation.

t = 0 · The Big Bang

The beginning of time and space

Not an explosion in space, but an expansion of space from an extremely hot, dense state. "What came before?" may not even be a well-posed question — time itself begins here. The Big Bang describes what happened after that initial moment, not the moment itself.

t ≈ 10⁻⁴³ s · Planck epoch

Where known physics breaks down

Gravity was as strong as the other forces and quantum effects dominated spacetime. We have no complete theory of quantum gravity, so this is the true edge of the map.

t ≈ 10⁻³⁴ s · Cosmic inflation

The universe balloons by 10²⁶×

A brief burst of exponential expansion stretched a subatomic patch to macroscopic size. Inflation elegantly explains why the universe looks so uniform and flat, and how quantum jitters became the seeds of galaxies. It remains our best theory of the beginning — though not yet proven.

t ≈ 10⁻⁶ s · Quark–hadron transition

Protons and neutrons condense

The quark–gluon plasma cooled enough for quarks to bind into protons and neutrons. Crucially, matter slightly outnumbered antimatter — by about one part in a billion. After nearly everything annihilated, that tiny leftover became all the matter that exists.

The first elements & the glowing fog

From seconds to hundreds of thousands of years — the era we can test directly against observation.

t ≈ 1 s · Neutrino decoupling

The universe's first "photograph"

Neutrinos stopped interacting and streamed free, forming a cosmic neutrino background that still permeates space today (though far too faint to detect directly, its imprint shows up in the CMB).

t ≈ 3 min · Big Bang nucleosynthesis

Hydrogen, helium, and a trace of lithium

For a few minutes the whole universe was a fusion reactor. It forged roughly 75% hydrogen and 25% helium by mass, plus a whisper of deuterium and lithium. The predicted abundances match observations to remarkable precision — one of the strongest pillars of the Big Bang model.

t ≈ 380,000 yr · Recombination

The universe becomes transparent

Until now, free electrons scattered light so densely that the cosmos was an opaque glowing fog. As it cooled below ~3,000 K, electrons combined with nuclei into neutral atoms. Light was suddenly free to travel — and that first free light is the cosmic microwave background we still detect today.

The dark ages and cosmic dawn

The long, dark stretch before the first stars — and the fireworks that ended it.

t ≈ 0.38–100 Myr · The cosmic dark ages

No stars yet

After the CMB faded, the universe was filled with cooling neutral hydrogen and no sources of light. Gravity quietly went to work, pulling matter (led by dark matter) into denser and denser clumps.

t ≈ 100–400 Myr · The first stars

Cosmic dawn ignites

The first stars — massive, hot, made of pristine hydrogen and helium — switched on inside collapsing halos. They forged the first heavier elements and, dying as supernovae, seeded space with carbon, oxygen, and iron. JWST is now catching galaxies from this era that look surprisingly bright and mature (see Mysteries).

t ≈ 0.4–1 Gyr · Reionization

The fog lifts a second time

Ultraviolet light from the first stars and galaxies stripped electrons back off the hydrogen filling space, making the intergalactic medium transparent to UV. This "reionization" was the universe's last major phase change.

Galaxies, the Sun, and now

Billions of years of assembly, ending with a familiar blue dot.

t ≈ 1–10 Gyr · Galaxy assembly

Structure grows

Galaxies merged and grew along a vast "cosmic web" of dark-matter filaments. Star formation peaked around t ≈ 3–4 Gyr ("cosmic noon"), when the universe was cranking out new stars faster than at any time before or since.

t ≈ 9.2 Gyr · The Sun forms

Our neighborhood assembles

About 4.6 billion years ago, a cloud of gas — already enriched by generations of dead stars — collapsed into the Sun and its planets. Nearly every atom heavier than helium in your body was forged inside an earlier star. You are, quite literally, recycled starlight.

t ≈ 9–14 Gyr · Dark energy takes over

The expansion accelerates

For roughly the last 5–6 billion years, dark energy has dominated the cosmic budget, and the expansion has been speeding up. Distant galaxies recede ever faster; most will eventually cross beyond our horizon and disappear from view forever.

t = 13.8 Gyr · Today

You are here

A brief, information-rich window in cosmic history when the sky is still full of visible galaxies and the CMB is still detectable. Future astronomers, billions of years hence, may see an empty sky and never guess the Big Bang happened at all.

How do we know all this?

Element abundances

The observed 75% H / 25% He ratio matches Big Bang nucleosynthesis predictions precisely — a fossil from the first three minutes.

The CMB

The 2.725 K afterglow and its tiny ripples pin down the age, geometry, and contents of the cosmos. More →

Redshift & expansion

The farther away a galaxy, the faster it recedes — the signature of a universe that grew from a hot, dense start. More →
