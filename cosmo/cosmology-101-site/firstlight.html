First Light & the CMB

The oldest light in existence is all around you right now. When the universe was 380,000 years old it cooled enough to become transparent, releasing a flash of light in every direction. Stretched by 13.8 billion years of expansion, that flash is now faint microwaves — the cosmic microwave background. It is the single richest source of information in all of cosmology.

A snapshot of the infant universe

Before recombination, the cosmos was an opaque, glowing plasma — free electrons scattered light like fog scatters headlights. When atoms formed, that fog cleared instantly and the light streamed free. We call the moment we're seeing the "surface of last scattering." It's a baby photo of the universe at 380,000 years old.

That light has been traveling ever since, its wavelength stretched roughly 1,100-fold by expansion — from hot visible/infrared glow down to microwaves at just 2.725 K above absolute zero. About 1% of the static on an old untuned analog TV was this cosmic afterglow.

Why the tiny ripples matter so much

The CMB is astonishingly smooth — the same temperature in every direction to one part in 100,000. But those minute variations are everything: they are the density differences that gravity later amplified into galaxies, clusters, and the cosmic web. Every structure you've ever seen grew from these seeds.

Before recombination, gravity pulling matter inward and radiation pushing it outward set up sound waves rippling through the plasma — literal acoustic oscillations. When the universe went transparent, those waves froze in place, leaving a characteristic pattern of hot and cold spots. Measuring the sizes of those spots (the "acoustic peaks") is how cosmologists read off the universe's vital statistics.

What the CMB tells usThe answer
Geometry of spaceFlat, to within ~0.4% — the universe is not curved like a sphere or saddle.
Age of the universe13.8 billion years.
How much ordinary matter~5% — matching Big Bang nucleosynthesis independently.
How much dark matter~27% — required to fit the pattern of peaks.
How much dark energy~68% — the remainder needed for a flat universe.
Three completely different lines of evidence — the CMB, element abundances, and galaxy surveys — all converge on the same recipe. That agreement is why cosmologists trust the strange 5 / 27 / 68 split even without knowing what most of it is.

The missions that mapped it

COBE · 1989

First detected the ripples and confirmed the near-perfect "blackbody" spectrum. Won the 2006 Nobel Prize.

WMAP · 2001

Sharpened the map and nailed down the cosmic recipe and a ~13.8-billion-year age.

Planck · 2009

The current gold standard — the most precise all-sky map, and the source of most numbers on this site.
