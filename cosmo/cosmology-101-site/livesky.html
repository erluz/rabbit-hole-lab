How to read an astronomy image

Color is often a choice

Deep-space images frequently map invisible wavelengths (infrared, X-ray) to visible colors. The "Pillars of Creation" you know are false-color — scientifically meaningful, but not what your eye would see.

Every point is a time machine

The farther the object, the older the light. A galaxy 10 billion light-years away shows you the universe as it was 10 billion years ago.

Redshift = distance

Astronomers label deep-field galaxies by redshift z. Higher z means farther, older, and — thanks to expansion — more stretched light.

The great cosmic eyes

🔭 James Webb Space Telescope

Infrared, launched 2021. Sees the earliest, most redshifted galaxies and peers through dust into stellar nurseries. Gallery →

🛰️ Hubble Space Telescope

Visible/UV, since 1990. Still delivering, and its Deep Field images revealed thousands of galaxies in a speck of "empty" sky. Gallery →

🌌 ESA Euclid

Launched 2023 to map billions of galaxies and probe dark energy across cosmic time. Mission →

📡 Planck (retired)

Mapped the cosmic microwave background with record precision — the source of most numbers on this site. Mission →
