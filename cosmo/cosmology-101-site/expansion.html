The Expanding Universe

Galaxies aren't flying through space away from us. The space between them is growing — like raisins drifting apart in rising dough, or dots on an inflating balloon. Every observer, anywhere, sees the same thing: everything receding, faster the farther it is. This single fact is the observational bedrock of the Big Bang.

Hubble's Law

In 1929, Edwin Hubble noticed that a galaxy's recession speed is proportional to its distance. Twice as far means twice as fast.

v = H₀ × d (recession velocity = Hubble constant × distance)

Run that backwards and everything was together at one moment — the Big Bang. The constant H₀ sets the current expansion rate and, roughly, the age of the universe. Its value (~67–73 km/s per megaparsec) is measured two different ways that stubbornly disagree — the Hubble Tension.

Scale factor a = 1.00 every galaxy sees itself at the center

Notice that nothing is at the "center" of the expansion. From every galaxy's point of view, all the others rush away — because space itself is what's stretching.

Redshift — the cosmic speedometer

As space stretches, so does the light traveling through it. Wavelengths get longer, shifting toward the red end of the spectrum. The more the light is stretched, the farther and longer it has traveled — so redshift is simultaneously a measure of distance, speed, and lookback time.

Cosmological redshift

Caused by the expansion of space itself while light is in transit. This is the dominant effect for distant galaxies and the one cosmologists mean by "redshift z."

Doppler redshift

The familiar effect from an object's own motion through space — like a receding siren dropping in pitch. Matters for nearby objects.

Gravitational redshift

Light climbing out of a strong gravitational field loses energy and reddens. Predicted by general relativity; tiny but measurable.

Key idea: when you look far away, you look back in time. A galaxy at redshift z = 1 shows you light that left when the universe was about 6 billion years old. Play with the redshift ↔ time converter on the Toolkit page.

Faster than light? Yes — and it's allowed

Beyond a certain distance, galaxies recede faster than the speed of light. This doesn't break relativity: nothing is moving through space faster than light. It's the space in between that's expanding, and there's no speed limit on how fast space itself can grow.

These galaxies are still visible — their ancient light left long ago and is still arriving. But we can never reach them or send them a signal; they've crossed our cosmic horizon. As dark energy accelerates the expansion, more and more of the universe is slipping permanently beyond reach.

A common misconception, cleared up

❌ "There was a center where the Big Bang exploded."

No. The Big Bang happened everywhere at once. Space itself was dense everywhere and has been expanding everywhere since. There's no special point and no edge you could fly to.

❌ "Galaxies are flying through space away from us."

Mostly no. Their recession is space stretching between us. Galaxies do have small local motions on top of that (Andromeda is actually heading toward us), but the overall flow is expansion.
