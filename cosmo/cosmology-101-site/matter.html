The Dark Universe

Here is the most humbling fact in all of science: everything humanity has ever seen, touched, or built — every star, planet, and person — is about 5% of what exists. The other 95% comes in two flavors of darkness we have named but never identified.

Dark energy · ~68%
Dark matter · ~27%
Ordinary matter · ~5%

And most of that 5% is thin intergalactic gas — stars and planets are a rounding error. The two darknesses are unrelated: dark matter pulls things together; dark energy pushes the whole universe apart.

Part 1

Dark matter — the invisible scaffolding

Dark matter is mass that doesn't emit, absorb, or reflect light. We can't see it directly, yet it outweighs all the stars and gas roughly five to one and forms the scaffolding on which every galaxy hangs. We infer it purely from its gravity.

The evidence is overwhelming

1 · Galaxy rotation curves

Stars at the edges of spiral galaxies orbit just as fast as those near the center. With only visible matter they should slow down like outer planets do. Extra unseen mass is holding them.

2 · Gravitational lensing

Galaxy clusters bend background light far more than their visible mass can account for. Mapping the bending maps the dark matter directly.

3 · The Bullet Cluster

In this cosmic collision, lensing shows the mass sailed straight through while the visible gas got stuck and heated — the "smoking gun" that the mass is something other than ordinary matter.

4 · The CMB & cosmic web

The pattern of ripples in the early-universe light, and the large-scale arrangement of galaxies, both require about five times more matter than we can see. More →

So what is it?

We know what dark matter does, not what it is. The leading idea is a new kind of particle that barely interacts with ordinary matter. Candidates include:

CandidateIdeaStatus
WIMPsWeakly Interacting Massive Particles — heavy, sluggish particles from beyond the Standard Model.Long the favorite; direct-detection experiments keep coming up empty, narrowing the options.
AxionsUltralight particles first proposed to fix an unrelated problem in particle physics.A hot current contender; several experiments are actively hunting them.
Primordial black holesBlack holes formed in the very early universe, not from dying stars.Possible for narrow mass ranges; heavily constrained by observations.
MACHOsDim ordinary objects — faint stars, rogue planets.Largely ruled out; there simply aren't enough of them.
Modified gravity (MOND)Maybe gravity itself behaves differently, and there's no dark matter at all.Explains individual galaxies well but struggles with clusters and the CMB.
Finding out what dark matter is would be one of the biggest discoveries in the history of physics. Despite decades of ingenious experiments, it has never been directly detected — only felt through gravity.
Part 2

Dark energy — the accelerator

In 1998, two teams measured distant supernovae expecting to see cosmic expansion slowing under gravity. Instead they found it speeding up. Something with negative pressure — a repulsive "energy of empty space" — is pushing the universe apart. They called it dark energy, and the discovery won the 2011 Nobel Prize.

Einstein's accidental idea

The simplest version is Einstein's cosmological constant (Λ): a fixed energy density baked into space itself. Add empty volume, add more of it. As the universe expands and matter thins out, this constant energy inevitably comes to dominate — which is exactly what happened a few billion years ago. Einstein introduced Λ for the wrong reason and later called it his "greatest blunder." It turned out to be one of his most prescient ideas.

Its behaviour is captured by one number, the equation of state: w = pressure / energy density.
A cosmological constant has exactly w = −1. Measuring whether w is truly −1 is a central goal of modern cosmology.

Big Freeze / Heat Death

And now the plot twist

Everyone assumed w = −1 exactly. But in 2024–2025, the DESI survey — mapping tens of millions of galaxies — found hints that dark energy may have been stronger in the past and is now weakening. Combined with supernova and CMB data, DESI's 2025 release disfavors a constant Λ at up to ~4σ. It's not yet the 5σ threshold for a discovery, but if it holds, the standard model of cosmology will need rewriting.
