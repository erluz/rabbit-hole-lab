Paradoxes & Mysteries

This is where cosmology stops being a textbook and becomes a frontier. Some of these are classic paradoxes with satisfying resolutions; others are live crises that could rewrite the standard model within our lifetimes. Tap any card to flip it, then read the deep dives below.

Paradox · 1823

Why is the night sky dark?

In an infinite, eternal, star-filled universe, every line of sight should end on a star — the whole sky should blaze like the Sun.

tap for the resolution →
Resolution

Because it had a beginning

The universe is finite in age, so light from very distant stars hasn't arrived yet — and expansion redshifts distant light out of view. Darkness itself is evidence of the Big Bang.

Crisis · active

The Hubble Tension

The early-universe expansion rate (~67.4) and the local one (~73) disagree at 5σ. Both are precise. Both can't be right in the standard model.

tap for the state of play →
2025 status

JWST made it worse

Webb confirmed the local Cepheid distances instead of debunking them, deepening the tension. It may be pointing to genuinely new physics.

Frontier · 2024–25

Is dark energy changing?

The standard model treats dark energy as constant. DESI now sees hints it was stronger in the past and is weakening.

tap for the numbers →
DESI DR2

A ~4σ hint

DESI 2025 + CMB + supernovae disfavor a constant Λ at up to ~4σ. Not yet the 5σ discovery bar — but if it holds, the textbook needs rewriting.

Paradox · 1950

Where is everybody?

The galaxy is old and vast, with billions of Earth-like worlds. A single civilization could cross it in a few million years. So why the silence?

tap for the candidates →
Possible answers

Take your pick

Maybe life is rare; maybe intelligence self-destructs (a "Great Filter"); maybe they're quiet, or we're simply early. We genuinely don't know.

Paradox · quantum

Black-hole information

Black holes evaporate. If they radiate featureless heat and vanish, the information about what fell in is destroyed — which quantum mechanics forbids.

tap for the twist →
Where it stands

Physics vs. physics

Relativity and quantum theory give contradictory answers. Recent work ("islands," the Page curve) suggests information escapes — but the full theory is missing.

Paradox · fine-tuning

The worst prediction in physics

Quantum theory predicts empty space should hold up to 10¹²⁰× more energy than the tiny dark-energy value we observe.

tap for the ideas →
Attempts

No accepted answer

From unknown cancellation mechanisms to the multiverse — that Λ varies across countless universes and we live in a rare hospitable one.

Paradox · origin

Why is there anything at all?

The Big Bang should have made equal matter and antimatter, which annihilate completely. Yet here we are.

tap for the search →
Baryogenesis

A one-in-a-billion excess

Something favored matter over antimatter. Experiments hunt for tiny asymmetries in particle decays, but the mechanism remains unknown.

Frontier · JWST

The "impossible" early galaxies

Webb keeps finding galaxies too big, bright, and mature to exist just a few hundred million years after the Big Bang.

Open question

Faster than we thought

Either early star formation was far more efficient than models allow, or some light comes from feeding black holes. An active puzzle, not (yet) a crisis.

Deep dive · 1

The Hubble Tension, in detail

Two rigorous ways of measuring how fast the universe expands give answers that don't match — and the gap has only sharpened as measurements improved. This is arguably the biggest crisis in cosmology today.

MethodHowH₀ (km/s/Mpc)
Early universe (Planck)Fit the CMB with the standard ΛCDM model and extrapolate forward.≈ 67.4
Local (SH0ES, HST+JWST)Build a "distance ladder" from Cepheid stars to Type Ia supernovae.≈ 73
JWST recalibration (Freedman 2025)Alternate local calibration using multiple distance indicators.≈ 70 (± ~1.5)

The early- and late-universe values differ at about the 5σ level — a one-in-3.5-million chance of being a fluke. Many hoped JWST would reveal an error in the local Cepheid measurements; instead it largely confirmed them. Possible explanations range from an unknown systematic error, to "early dark energy" that briefly acted before recombination, to something genuinely missing from the standard model. As of 2026 it remains unresolved.

Why it matters: H₀ is woven into nearly every cosmic distance and age. If the tension is real new physics, it touches everything downstream.
Deep dive · 2

Is dark energy evolving?

The standard model (ΛCDM) assumes dark energy is a constant — the same density for all time (equation of state w = −1). It's the simplest assumption, and until recently the data fit it fine.

Then the Dark Energy Spectroscopic Instrument (DESI) began mapping the 3D positions of tens of millions of galaxies, using "baryon acoustic oscillations" — the frozen sound waves from the early universe (see First Light) — as a cosmic ruler stamped across billions of years. In 2024, and more strongly in its 2025 Data Release 2, DESI found that the data fit slightly better if dark energy was stronger in the past and is weakening now. Combined with CMB and supernova data, a constant Λ is disfavored at up to ~4σ depending on which supernova sample is used.

The honest caveat: 4σ is a strong hint, not a discovery — physics reserves that for 5σ. The result also depends on which datasets are combined. But if it holds up with more data, it would be the first crack in the cosmological constant since Einstein, and a genuine revolution.
Deep dive · 3

The cosmological constant problem

Quantum field theory says empty space is not empty — it seethes with virtual particles that should contribute energy. When physicists estimate how much, they get a number up to 10¹²⁰ times larger than the tiny dark-energy density we actually observe. That's a 1 followed by 120 zeros of disagreement — often called the worst quantitative prediction in the history of physics.

Nobody knows why the observed value is so absurdly small but not exactly zero. Proposed answers include an unknown symmetry that almost-but-not-quite cancels the energy, or the anthropic multiverse idea: that the constant takes different values across a vast ensemble of universes, and only in the rare ones where it's small enough can galaxies — and observers — form. Neither is confirmed; this remains one of the deepest puzzles at the border of cosmology and particle physics.

Deep dive · 4

JWST and the early universe

The James Webb Space Telescope, peering deeper into the past than anything before it, keeps finding galaxies at redshifts z > 10 — within a few hundred million years of the Big Bang — that appear more numerous, luminous, and massive than pre-launch models predicted. Headlines occasionally claim this "breaks the Big Bang." It does not.

What it does challenge is our understanding of how quickly stars and galaxies assembled. The leading explanations are mundane by cosmic standards: early star formation may have been unusually efficient, some of the light may come from actively feeding black holes rather than stars, or our models of early stellar populations need adjusting. It's a vibrant, fast-moving research area reshaping the picture of cosmic dawn — a puzzle within the Big Bang framework, not a refutation of it.

The takeaway. Roughly 95% of the universe is unidentified, two independent measurements of its expansion disagree, and the leading theory of empty space is off by 120 orders of magnitude. Cosmology is not a finished subject — it's one of the most exciting open frontiers in all of science, and some of the biggest answers may arrive within the next decade.
