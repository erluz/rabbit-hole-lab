A 1994 experiment · examined honestly

Two brains, one signal? Reading a famous claim carefully.

In 1994, four researchers reported that when two people meditated together and were then sealed in separate shielded rooms 14.5 m apart, flashing a light at one brain produced a matching electrical blip in the other — with no signal passing between them. They called it quantum entanglement of human brains. Here's exactly what they did, what they found, and — just as important — whether it holds up.

01 / The physics being borrowed

Entanglement is real — but it can't send a message

The paper leans on one of the strangest facts in physics. To judge the claim fairly, you need to know what entanglement actually does, and the one thing it famously cannot do.

⇌

What Einstein, Podolsky & Rosen noticed (1935)

Two quantum particles can be "entangled" so that measuring one instantly tells you about the other, no matter how far apart they are. Einstein called it "spooky action at a distance." Experiments (Aspect, 1982) confirmed the correlations are real — nature really is nonlocal in this narrow sense.

✕

The catch: no information travels

Entanglement produces matching random outcomes — but you can't control them, so you can't use them to send a signal. This is a proven result called the no-communication theorem. It's why entanglement never breaks Einstein's cosmic speed limit. Hold onto this: it becomes decisive later.

A fair analogy

Imagine two magic coins that always land the same way as each other — but each lands randomly. Flip them a thousand miles apart and the results match perfectly. Spooky? Yes. Can you send a message with them? No — because neither of you can choose the outcome. That gap between "correlated" and "communicating" is the crux of this whole story.

02 / The hypothesis on trial

Could a brain be a quantum object?

The researchers were testing a bold, decades-old speculation: that the brain isn't just a classical biological machine, but hosts a large-scale quantum system — and that two brains, once "connected," could become entangled like particles.

?

The quantum-mind idea

A minority of thinkers (Penrose, Hameroff, Stapp, Goswami) have proposed consciousness involves quantum processes. It's a real, if fringe, research thread — not mainstream neuroscience.

∴

The leap this paper makes

If brains are quantum, maybe two people who "connect" become one entangled system — so measuring one (a flash) would collapse the other, even at a distance.

◎

Why it's testable

Entanglement can't be faked by any classical system, so a genuine nonlocal correlation between brains would be strong evidence. The logic is sound — the question is whether the data deliver.

Taking it seriously: this is a legitimate question. Quantum effects do appear in biology (photosynthesis, bird navigation). The honest issue is not "how dare they ask" — it's whether the specific evidence supports the specific, extraordinary conclusion. Let's look.

04 / The result they reported

The "transferred potential"

In roughly one pair in four — the ones judged to have "connected" — a shape resembling Subject A's flash response appeared in isolated Subject B's averaged EEG. In controls, it didn't. That's the whole claim in one picture.

If this were solid, it would be revolutionary — direct evidence of macroscopic entanglement between living brains, a genuine mind-to-mind physical link, and a crack in our picture of the brain as a classical organ. Extraordinary claims like that earn extraordinary scrutiny. So let's apply it.

05 / Does it hold up? — the statistics

How to find a "signal" in pure noise

The analysis computed 48 correlations per pair — sliding a 16-sample window across the trace — and highlighted the windows that scored high. Here's why that's a problem. Below are two streams of completely random noise. Watch what happens when we scan them the same way.

Two unrelated random signals routinely produce an eye-catching correlation in some window if you test enough of them. Reporting that window as "the effect" — without correcting for the 47 you also tried — is a classic way to manufacture significance from nothing. This doesn't prove the paper's result was noise; it shows the method can't rule noise out.

Scrutiny

Tiny sample

7 pairs, and only ~25% "connected" — so the headline effect rests on roughly two pairs. Far too few to be sure of anything.

Scrutiny

Rescaled to match

The evoked and transferred traces were plotted on different amplitude scales — the paper's own caption flags it. Similarity after independent rescaling is weak evidence.

Scrutiny

Ordinary EEG artifacts

Eye movements, muscle tension, shared electrical environment and a common reference can all create look-alike correlations between two recordings.

06 / Does it hold up? — the physics

Why physicists doubt the mechanism

Even setting the statistics aside, the proposed explanation — sustained quantum entanglement between two warm, wet brains meters apart — runs into a wall. Quantum states in the brain don't survive long enough to matter. Tap a marker.

Tap any marker above to see what happens on that timescale.
Physics

Decoherence is brutally fast

Max Tegmark's 2000 calculation puts quantum "survival time" in the brain at about 10⁻¹³–10⁻²⁰ seconds — roughly ten or more orders of magnitude shorter than the ~10⁻³–10⁻¹ s of actual neural events. Coherence dies long before a single neuron even fires.

Physics

The no-communication theorem, again

Even if two brains were entangled, entanglement can't carry a signal. So a "transferred potential" that reproduces the flash response would require sending information — which the paper's own quantum framework forbids. Its speculation about "Morse code" over this channel isn't allowed by the physics it invokes.

In fairness: quantum biology is a real and active field, and a minority (e.g. Penrose–Hameroff) still argue the brain-decoherence numbers aren't settled. But even the most optimistic proposals concern fleeting effects in single molecules — not stable entanglement linking two whole brains across a room after they've only interacted by talking and sitting together.

07 / What happened next

Did anyone reproduce it?

A real effect should show up again in other labs. The follow-up record is the most telling part of the story — and it's mixed at best.

2002 · SCHMIDT et al.
Large, well-powered replication
Designed to catch even a small effect. It found nothing — a clean null result.
2003 · WACKERMANN et al. (Freiburg)
Partial, cautious signal
Could not reproduce the "transferred potential" itself, but saw subtle EEG fluctuations between separated subjects. They explicitly said nothing in their data supports a quantum-physical origin.
2004–2008 · Various
Contested follow-ups
Some EEG/fMRI "connectedness" reports appeared — small, inconsistent, and criticized for artifacts and analysis choices. Control groups sometimes trended "significant" too — a red flag.

Where it lives in the literature

Most follow-up work appears in parapsychology and fringe-physics venues, not mainstream neuroscience or physics journals. The original itself ran in Physics Essays, a journal known for speculative content and light peer review.

The honest summary

After 30 years, the specific "transferred potential" has not been robustly replicated; the strongest, best-powered attempt found nothing; and even sympathetic replicators declined to endorse the quantum explanation. It remains outside accepted science.

Why "no distance fall-off" isn't proof

The authors read the lack of attenuation as nonlocality. But a statistical artifact doesn't fade with distance either. Absence of fall-off is equally consistent with there being no real signal to fall off.

08 / The transferable skill

A checklist for any extraordinary claim

This is the real takeaway. Whenever a study claims something that would rewrite the textbooks, run it through these questions. Here's how this paper scores.

Is the evidence as extraordinary as the claim?

Rewriting physics needs more than a suggestive result from ~2 subject pairs.

Weak

Was it pre-registered / analysis fixed in advance?

Choosing which of 48 correlation windows to feature after seeing the data invites false positives.

No

Is there a plausible mechanism?

Sustained brain-to-brain entanglement conflicts with decoherence and the no-communication theorem.

Implausible

Have independent labs replicated it?

The best-powered attempt found nothing; others are mixed and contested.

Not robustly

Were mundane explanations ruled out?

EEG artifacts, shared environment, small samples and multiple comparisons aren't fully excluded.

No

Is the venue rigorously peer-reviewed?

Physics Essays is speculative and lightly reviewed, not a mark of strong vetting.

No

None of this requires assuming bad faith. Good scientists can produce results that don't survive scrutiny — that's why replication and independent checks exist. The checklist is the immune system of science.

09 / The man behind the claim

Who was Jacobo Grinberg-Zylberbaum?

The transferred-potential paper wasn't a one-off stunt. It was a late chapter in the career of an ambitious, prolific, and genuinely unusual Mexican scientist — one whose story runs from mainstream neuroscience to the far edge of the paranormal, and ends in an unsolved mystery.

His grand idea: the Syntergic Theory

Over some fifteen years Grinberg built what he called "syntergy" (from synthesis + energy). He proposed that the brain's combined neuronal activity — a "neuronal field" — interacts with a fundamental, invisible structure of space he named the lattice (or matrix). In this view, the reality we perceive is co-created where field meets lattice, and consciousness is an active participant, not a passive observer.

Where the idea came from

His thinking sits in a real, if heterodox, lineage: David Bohm's "implicate order" and Karl Pribram's holonomic (holographic) model of the brain. Serious scientists floated those ideas — Grinberg pushed them much further than most were willing to go.

Mainstream

EEG & communication

His earlier, credible work measured EEG coherence and "interhemispheric correlation" between people communicating — the empirical seed the transferred-potential study grew from.

Fringe

Shamans & Pachita

He studied Mexican healers, most famously the psychic "surgeon" Pachita (Bárbara Guerrero), interpreting her rituals through his neuronal-field framework and devoting a book to her.

Fringe

"Extraocular vision"

He also reported studying children who could supposedly perceive without their eyes — a striking claim that, like the transferred potential, never gained independent support.

The disappearance — fact vs. legend

What's documented: In December 1994 — accounts differ on the exact day, some citing December 8, others around his birthday on the 12th — Grinberg vanished from Mexico City. He was never found, and the case remains officially unsolved. A 2020 documentary, Ida Cuéllar's The Secret of Dr. Grinberg, revived public interest and explores the puzzle with care.

What's legend: Popular retellings claim he had "proven telepathy," was abducted by the CIA for his research, or literally "transcended the physical plane." These make a gripping story, but they're speculation — and several trace back to books and posts written precisely to sell that mystery.

An unsolved disappearance is not evidence

However haunting the story, it says nothing about whether the transferred potential is real. A scientific claim stands or falls on data and replication, not on the drama surrounding its author. Holding those two apart is itself part of thinking clearly about this work.

The honest picture: a talented neurophysiologist with real early credentials who increasingly chased extraordinary claims his methods couldn't support. That mix — genuine ability, genuine curiosity, and evidence that didn't hold up — is exactly what makes his story worth understanding rather than mocking.

10 / The bigger picture

The real science of consciousness

Here's the twist: the transferred potential is fringe, but the question it reached for — how does a lump of brain produce inner experience? — is one of the deepest in all of science, and it's being pursued right now with real rigor. Seeing what that looks like is the whole lesson, because the contrast is stark.

The "hard problem"

Philosopher David Chalmers named it in 1995: we can explain how the brain processes information, but why any of it is accompanied by subjective experience — the redness of red, the sting of pain — remains genuinely unexplained. Nobody has solved it. That open door is what makes the field magnetic — and what fringe theories rush to fill.

GWT

Global Workspace

Consciousness is information "broadcast" widely across the brain, made available to many systems at once. (Baars, Dehaene)

IIT

Integrated Information

Consciousness is how unified and irreducible a system's information is — a quantity called "phi." (Tononi, Koch)

HOT

Higher-Order

A state is conscious when the brain represents that it is having that state. (Rosenthal, Lau)

RPT

Recurrent Processing

Awareness arises when signals loop back through sensory areas, not just feed forward. (Lamme)

How to test a hard claim properly

The 2025 adversarial collaboration

In a landmark study in Nature (2025), rival camps did something unusual: proponents of two leading theories (Global Workspace and IIT) sat down together, agreed in advance on exactly what each theory predicted, and pre-registered the whole experiment before collecting a single data point — 256 participants, measured three ways (fMRI, MEG, and intracranial EEG).

The result? No knockout. Some findings fit IIT — conscious content was decodable from the back of the brain, with no necessary "broadcast" from the front — while others challenged both theories. The field advanced not by one side winning, but by rigorous, honest testing.

Now compare 1994: seven subject pairs, no pre-registration, 48 correlation windows scanned after the fact, in a lenient journal. Same big question — opposite methods. That gap is why one is science and the other a curiosity.

So where does "quantum mind" fit?

Quantum theories of consciousness (like Penrose & Hameroff's "Orch-OR") do exist and are seriously debated — but they're a minority position, and even their boldest versions concern fleeting effects inside single molecules, not two whole brains entangled across a room. Grinberg's claim sits further out still. Taking consciousness seriously does not require believing the transferred potential is real.

A note of humility that says it all: in 1998 the neuroscientist Christof Koch bet the philosopher David Chalmers that the brain mechanism of consciousness would be pinned down within 25 years. In 2023 Koch conceded and paid up — with a case of fine wine. The serious scientists know how hard this is, which is exactly why they're so careful.

11 / Bottom line

So — did two brains share a signal?

Not established

Almost certainly not in the way the paper claims.

The experiment asked a legitimate, fascinating question and attempted a genuine test. But the evidence is thin (a couple of subject pairs), the statistics are vulnerable to finding patterns in noise, the proposed quantum mechanism conflicts with well-established physics, and three decades of follow-up have not produced a robust replication. The consensus of mainstream neuroscience and physics is that no brain-to-brain nonlocal signal has been demonstrated.

What it is good for is exactly what you've just done: a vivid, self-contained case study in how to take a startling claim seriously and hold it to the standards that separate discovery from wishful pattern-matching. That skill outlasts any single paper.
