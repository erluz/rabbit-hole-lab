The one-minute briefing

What actually makes a penguin a penguin

Wings that row, not fly

Every penguin's wings have flattened into stiff flippers. They literally "fly" underwater, flapping to generate thrust rather than gliding through air.

Southern by nature

Native only to the Southern Hemisphere — from the Antarctic ice to temperate coasts. Only the Galápagos penguin lives on the equator, kept cool by chilly ocean currents.

Dressed for freezing water

Short, overlapping, oiled feathers packed tighter than any other bird's, plus a layer of blubber. Together they supply most of a penguin's insulation on land and at sea.

Not all penguins are creatures of ice. Larger species tend to live in the coldest places, while smaller ones spread into temperate and even tropical waters. Some ancestors were giants — fossil penguins from tens of millions of years ago reached the size of an adult human, and one recently described species may have weighed around 150 kg. Six genera, one family

The penguin family tree

All living penguins sit in a single family split into six branches. Broadly, the biggest and coldest-adapted are the Aptenodytes; the crested penguins (Eudyptes) are the most species-rich; and the banded Spheniscus reach furthest into warm water.

Courtship to crèche

The penguin life cycle

Most penguins are colonial and seasonally monogamous, returning to the same patch — and often the same partner — year after year. Nests range from pebble rings to burrows to no nest at all.

The emperor's winter

Emperors are the only bird that breeds during the Antarctic winter. The female lays a single egg, passes it to the male, and heads to sea to feed. For about 65 days he balances the egg on his feet under a fold of belly skin, eating nothing, huddling with thousands of other males against −40°C winds. When the chick hatches, the female returns with food — and the exhausted male finally walks to the ocean to break his fast. Young then gather in nursery groups called crèches for warmth and safety.

A southern world

Where penguins live

Penguins ring the Southern Hemisphere. They aren't only polar animals — cold currents carry the food that lets them live along temperate coasts of Africa, South America and Australasia, and even at the equator.

True Antarcticans — emperor & Adélie breed on the continent and its pack ice.
Sub-Antarctic islanders — king, macaroni, rockhoppers and royal crowd remote southern islands.
Temperate coasts — African, Humboldt, Magellanic and little penguins live on mild shores, following cold, fish-rich currents.
The equator — the Galápagos penguin is the only species that lives (barely) north of the line, chilled by the Humboldt and Cromwell currents.

Why can penguins live in the tropics but never crossed into the Northern Hemisphere? The warm equatorial ocean is a barrier they've never breached — even the Galápagos penguin only survives thanks to cold upwelling water. Attempts by people to introduce penguins up north have always failed to establish.

Sentinels of the Southern Ocean

Conservation

Most penguin populations are declining. Because they sit near the top of marine food webs, their trouble is an early warning about the health of the oceans themselves.

How the 18 species break down by risk

First of its kind · 2024

The African penguin is now Critically Endangered

In late 2024 the African penguin became the first penguin ever listed as Critically Endangered by the IUCN — one step from extinct-in-the-wild. Its population has collapsed by roughly 97%: from over a million birds a century ago to fewer than 10,000 breeding pairs. The main drivers are competition with commercial fishing for the sardines and anchovies it eats, climate-shifted prey, oil spills, and — historically — the removal of the guano it once burrowed into to nest. Conservation groups have gone to court to win no-fishing zones around key colonies.

The main threats

Reasons for hope

The 1959 Antarctic Treaty protects Antarctic species from hunting and disturbance. Marine protected areas and no-take fishing zones are expanding. Rescue and rehabilitation centres hand-rear abandoned chicks and clean oiled birds. Artificial nest boxes are boosting breeding success, and a few populations — some gentoo colonies among them — are actually growing as they shift range. Penguins are well-studied and much-loved, which makes them powerful ambassadors for ocean protection.

Conservation statuses follow the IUCN Red List. Most penguin assessments date from 2020; the African penguin was uplisted in 2024. Categories can change as new data arrives — always check the current Red List for the latest.
