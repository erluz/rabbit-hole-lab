01 / The word in the name

Fungible vs non-fungible

"Non-fungible" just means not interchangeable. One dollar is exactly like any other dollar — swap them and nothing changes; that's fungible. A specific painting, ticket, or collectible is one of a kind — that's non-fungible. NFTs bring that second idea to the digital world.

02 / Under the hood

What an NFT actually is

Strip away the hype and an NFT is just a row in a smart contract: a unique token ID, an owner's address, and a link to some metadata. That's it. The blockchain guarantees only one owner and a permanent history of every transfer. Below is the on-chain record for the token in the minter above.

04 / Bringing it to life

Minting: how a token is born

"Minting" is the moment a smart contract writes your token onto the blockchain for the first time, assigning it an ID and an owner. It costs a network fee ("gas"). After that, it can be transferred and traded forever.

1

Create & upload

Make the asset and put it somewhere durable (ideally IPFS/Arweave), plus a metadata file.

2

Deploy / call contract

A smart contract mints the token, recording its ID and pointing tokenURI at the metadata.

3

Pay gas

You pay a network fee to the validators who write the transaction into a block.

4

You own token #N

The blockchain now records you as the owner. It's yours to hold, sell or transfer.

Tip: "lazy minting" defers the on-chain write (and the gas) until the first sale, so creators can list for free — the buyer pays gas at purchase.

05 / The rulebooks

Token standards

A "standard" is a shared blueprint so that every wallet and marketplace can understand your token. On Ethereum, two dominate. (Other chains have their own — Solana's Metaplex, Bitcoin's Ordinals.)

06 / Where they trade

Marketplaces & the royalty story

Marketplaces are the storefronts. They differ by chain, audience and — controversially — how they treat creator royalties (a cut the artist earns on every resale). Once optional-by-default, royalties are now a genuine debate; on-chain enforcement (via tools like Manifold) is the workaround.

07 / Beyond the JPEG

What NFTs are actually used for

Profile pictures made NFTs famous, but "a unique, ownable, programmable token" turns out to be useful for a lot more than art.

◈

Art & PFPs

1-of-1 artwork and generative collections like CryptoPunks — identity and status in one token.

▶

Game items

Weapons, skins and land you truly own and can carry between games or sell freely.

🎟

Tickets & access

Event tickets and "token-gated" memberships — hold the token, unlock the door.

🌐

Domains & identity

Names like alice.eth are NFTs — a human-readable, ownable web3 identity.

♪

Music & media

Musicians sell releases and royalties directly to fans, skipping the middlemen.

🏠

Real-world assets

Deeds, certificates and tokenized property — early, but a fast-growing frontier.

08 / How we got here

A short history of NFTs

From an obscure experiment to a $17-billion frenzy and back to something quieter and more useful.

2012–2014
Colored Coins & "Quantum"
Early experiments tag assets on Bitcoin; Kevin McCoy mints "Quantum" (2014), often called the first NFT.
JUN 2017
CryptoPunks
Larva Labs releases 10,000 unique pixel characters — free to claim. The first PFP collection.
NOV 2017
CryptoKitties & ERC-721
Dapper Labs' breedable cats clog Ethereum and inspire the ERC-721 standard. The term "NFT" is coined.
MAR 2021
Beeple at Christie's
"Everydays: The First 5000 Days" sells for $69.3M — NFTs hit global headlines overnight.
APR 2021
Bored Ape Yacht Club
Yuga Labs' apes turn PFPs into celebrity-backed culture. Trading explodes from $82M (2020) to $17B (2021).
2022
The crash
The bubble bursts; monthly sales fall 90%+ from the peak. Speculation cools, builders stay.
2025–2026
Into the museums
CryptoPunks IP moves to a nonprofit; MoMA adds Punks to its permanent collection. Focus shifts to utility.
09 / Read this before you buy

Myths, risks & scams

NFTs are a real technology wrapped in a lot of noise. Knowing what's true — and what's dangerous — is the difference between a fun hobby and an expensive lesson.

Myth

"Right-click save = I own it now"

You copied the image, not the token. Ownership is the on-chain record, which your copy doesn't have. (Whether that record is worth anything is a separate question.)

Myth

"The blockchain stores my art forever"

Only if the metadata and image are on-chain or on IPFS/Arweave. If they sit on a company's server, they can vanish — the token would point to nothing.

True risk

Wallet-drainer scams

Fake mint sites and malicious "sign this" prompts can empty your wallet in one click. Verify URLs, never share your seed phrase, and be skeptical of free airdrops.

True risk

Rug pulls

A team hypes a collection, sells out the mint, then abandons it. Research the creators and treat anonymous "guaranteed returns" as a red flag.

True risk

Wash trading & fake volume

Bad actors trade with themselves to fake demand and inflate "floor prices." High volume isn't the same as real interest.

True risk

Brutal illiquidity

Prices are volatile and buyers can vanish. Many collections lost 80–95% of value. Never spend more than you can afford to lose.

This is education, not financial advice. NFTs can go to zero; treat any purchase as discretionary spending, not an investment strategy.
